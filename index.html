The Science Behind
Standing Waves

Sound inside a flute bounces between its open ends, creating two waves traveling in opposite directions. Their superposition forms a standing wave — with fixed, glowing antinodes and dark, still nodes.

〰️ STANDING WAVE
Formed when two identical waves travel in opposite directions and interfere. The pattern appears stationary — it doesn't move. Inside a flute, reflected sound waves create this beautiful phenomenon, locking energy at specific positions.
● NODE — Zero Displacement
Points where particles never move. Destructive interference keeps displacement permanently at zero. In a sound wave, nodes are points of maximum pressure variation. On the Lumina Flute, the LED here stays dim.
◎ ANTINODE — Maximum Displacement
Points of maximum oscillation — particles vibrate with greatest amplitude here. In a flute's open ends, antinodes always form. Pressure stays near atmospheric (constant). On the Lumina Flute, the LED here glows brightest!

// THE PROJECT

Lumina Flute
by S4F5

A 700 mm bamboo bansuri flute embedded with LEDs, a microphone sensor, and an Arduino UNO. When played, it maps sound vibrations to light — visualizing nodes (dim LEDs) and antinodes (bright LEDs) in real time.

Designed for students aged 14–18 who struggle with abstract wave physics. Traditional methods rely on static diagrams — the Lumina Flute makes the invisible, visible.
